# Writing

## Authority

This file defines strict rules for how agents produce written content: documentation, blog posts, presentations, reports, messages, and any other prose.

These rules apply to all writing tasks unless another rule file explicitly overrides them with equal or higher specificity.

## Audience

The default audience is technically literate professionals. Assume expertise. Do not explain concepts the audience already knows.

If the user specifies a different audience, adapt accordingly. This default applies when no audience is stated.

## Grammar and Punctuation

Prefer US English spelling. Avoid US idioms.

Em dashes are prohibited. Restructure sentences to avoid them. No exceptions and no substitutes: not commas, dots, or brackets.

Ellipses are prohibited.

Parentheses are prohibited unless removing them changes the meaning.

All numbers must be written as digits, with one exception: write out "a" or "an" instead of "1" when it functions as an indefinite article before a singular noun.

Only straight quotes are allowed. Curly quotes are prohibited.

Compound sentences joining two independent clauses with a coordinating conjunction require a comma before the conjunction.

Use commas after introductory phrases and clauses.

Articles (a, an, the) must be used where grammatically required. Omitting articles is prohibited unless deliberately creating a specific stylistic effect.

Parallel structure is required in lists and series. All items must follow the same grammatical form.

## Sentences

Vary sentence length deliberately. Short sentences for emphasis. Longer sentences when layering detail or connecting related ideas.

Do not chain short declarative statements together. When ideas are related, combine them into compound sentences that make the relationship explicit.

Average sentence length should stay between 12 and 20 words. Sentences under 10 words are for emphasis or rhythm resets. Sentences over 25 words should be rare and justified.

## Paragraphs

Paragraphs must be short. Standard paragraphs run 3 to 5 sentences. Very short paragraphs of 1 to 2 sentences may be used for impact or transition. Paragraphs over 6 sentences are prohibited.

Each paragraph must make a single point. If it covers two ideas, split it.

When a paragraph feels too long, the solution is not to shorten it by cutting content. Split it into multiple paragraphs and connect them.

## Transitions and Flow

Every paragraph must connect to the one before it. No paragraph may stand alone or introduce a new idea without building from what precedes it.

Transitions must be purposeful and integrated into the paragraph openings. Do not use generic connective phrases ("Furthermore," "Additionally," "Moreover," "In addition"). Weave the connection into the substance of the sentence.

The reader should never feel a gap between paragraphs. If removing a paragraph would leave no visible seam, the surrounding paragraphs were not properly connected.

## Structure

Lead with the most important point. A reader who stops after the first paragraph should still take away the core message.

Use headings to create scannable structure in anything longer than a few paragraphs. Headings must describe the content of the section, not label it generically ("Overview," "Details," "Miscellaneous").

Do not rename a heading the user wrote unless the user explicitly asked for a rename. APA title case corrections are allowed; changing the words is not.

Do not use horizontal rules as section separators.

## Formatting

Use bold sparingly and only when a term or phrase must stand out on a skim. Do not bold entire sentences or use bold as a substitute for a heading.

Italics are prohibited unless explicitly requested.

Titles and headings must follow APA title case: capitalize the first word, the last word, and all major words. Do not capitalize minor words (and, but, or, nor, for, so, yet, a, an, the, in, on, at, to, by, of, with, from) unless they are the first or last word.

Use bullet points only for sets of independent, parallel items. Do not convert explanatory or sequential prose into bullet lists. Lists are allowed only when they improve clarity; overuse is prohibited.

Inline code formatting (backticks) is for identifiers, commands, file paths, and literal values. Do not use backticks for emphasis or for ordinary words.

Code fences must specify a language when the content is code. Use plain fences (no language tag) for ASCII trees, directory structures, and other non-code structured text.

## Tone

Be direct, reflective, and analytical. Show curiosity and critical reasoning rather than authority for its own sake.

"Direct" does not mean clipped or stripped of personality. It means no hedging, no filler, and no delay in reaching the point. Warmth, nuance, and conversational touches are welcome when they are natural.

Do not hedge with phrases like "it should be noted that," "it is important to mention," or "it is worth considering." State the point.

Do not use filler: "in order to" (use "to"), "at the end of the day," "as a matter of fact," "the fact that," "in terms of."

Do not use slogans, performative enthusiasm, or motivational language.

Cultural references are welcome when they reinforce an idea naturally. They must never feel decorative or forced.

## Repetition

Repetition of concepts, words, or phrases is prohibited unless deliberate for rhetorical effect. This applies at the sentence, paragraph, and section level.

If a word appears more than twice in a short passage without rhetorical purpose, find a different construction.

## Preservation

When rewriting or refining the user's text, preserve the original tone, style, and information. Do not make the text more formal, more casual, or more concise than the original unless explicitly asked to.

Do not remove sentiment, softening language, or conversational touches that the user included deliberately. If something looks like a stylistic choice, treat it as one.

If the original text is already well written, say so. Do not manufacture changes.

## Edit Scope

When asked to fix a phrase, change only that phrase. Do not rewrite surrounding sentences, paragraphs, or sections. The smallest edit that addresses the request is the correct one.

Do not add sentences, sections, or paragraphs the user did not ask for. Scope creep in prose is the same violation as scope creep in code.

Violation: the user asks to fix a phrase on line 12, and the agent rewrites lines 10 through 15 to "improve flow."

## Accuracy

Every claim must be supportable. Do not state something as fact unless it is established knowledge or explicitly sourced. When uncertain, say so.

Do not invent citations, statistics, quotes, or attributions. If the agent cannot verify a claim, it must either omit it or flag it as unverified for the user to confirm.
